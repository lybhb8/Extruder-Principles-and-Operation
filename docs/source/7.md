# 单螺杆挤出机中的固体输送与熔融



# 7.1 固体输送与熔融的相关性

挤出机不仅是熔体输送装置，其特性与产能已在第6章讨论。若挤出物未能完全熔融或流动不稳，优化挤出机的输送能力可能毫无意义。因此，固体输送与熔融性能对决定整机运行表现至关重要。

粉状与颗粒状固体的流动受颗粒几何形状、颗粒间及颗粒与金属表面间的摩擦力，以及水分等附加参数影响。与挤出机性能直接相关的固体输送过程有三种：料斗内的重力流动、料口向螺杆通道的填充，以及沿螺杆的输送。

重力流动过程中，料斗内物料整体会形成压力分布曲线。料斗底部的压力波动（由固体柱高度变化或流动阻滞引起）将导致挤出速率、能耗及熔体温度的波动。料斗最严重的问题是因颗粒间摩擦显著增大导致的局部或完全堵塞，本章后文将对此进行探讨。为克服这些困难，已采用机械解决方案：如料斗振动、轻柔搅拌进料及强制进料（利用螺杆输送颗粒并产生压力）。料斗的排料能力同样决定着挤出机的最大可能产量。

螺杆填充状态同样影响工艺性能。进料喉部需具备良好冷却能力，避免颗粒粘附壁面影响流动稳定性。若进料斗衬板与加工物料不匹配，可能导致物料被挤回进料喉部，阻碍新料流入。通常进料口设计为偏移或切向螺杆布置。橡胶挤出工艺中，粗大团聚物可能堵塞进料喉部。因此进料口常设置在螺杆下降侧的切线位置，而上升侧的螺杆则切入下降的物料柱。实验（Hayward, 1977）表明，采用常规与改良型进料槽衬板（图7.1及表7.1）时，两者性能差异甚微。

两种设计在处理自由流动固体时的流量表现相当。然而，当处理流动性差的物料时，改进型设计展现出明显更高且更稳定的输送效率。

在螺杆内部，颗粒物很快被压缩成固体料柱，该料柱借助内筒壁产生的摩擦力向前输送，而螺杆表面的摩擦力则起到阻滞作用。关键在于确保筒壁摩擦系数远高于螺杆表面系数，如此即使物料摩擦特性偶有波动，亦不会显著影响系统稳定性。实现高筒壁摩擦的常用方法是加工纵向或螺旋形沟槽。

固体输送区的重要性通常取决于所选操作条件。当操作由熔体输送能力控制时，固体输送仅限于螺杆前几圈，导致压力升幅较小。若料筒冷却效率高，较长的固体输送区将产生较大压力，从而提升挤出机的输送能力（Broyer and Tadmor, 1972）。

单螺杆挤出过程中形成的熔融机制可能因材料而异，即使相同材料在不同操作条件下也会变化。所有情况下，固体床（或多或少被熔融材料包围）都可能过早断裂，从而引发压力波动。对这一现象重要性的认识促使了隔离螺杆的发展，该设计通过将熔池与固体床分离来确保后者的完整性。在特定螺杆几何结构和操作条件下，熔融过程的完成需要螺杆通道具备精确定义的螺旋长度。若该长度过长，则无法实现真正的熔体输送（从而同时影响压力生成和分布式混合过程），螺杆输送的聚合物很可能存在大量未熔融和混合不均的现象。在此情况下，熔融过程将限制工艺产量，可能需要采取特殊操作策略。

实验已证实固体输送与熔融过程及其相互作用对工艺性能和稳定性的关键影响。稳定性的先决条件之一是满足以下条件：



$$
Q _ {\mathrm {s}} \geq Q _ {\mathrm {m}} \geq Q _ {\mathrm {p}} \tag {7.1}
$$

其中 $Q_{\mathrm{s}}$、$Q_{\mathrm{m}}$ 和 $Q_{\mathrm{p}}$ 分别表示固体输送区、熔融区和泵送功能区的处理能力。不稳定现象通常根据其发生频率进行区分，可分为周期性或随机性，其频率可能高于、等于或低于螺杆旋转频率。频率高于螺杆转速的不稳定现象（如鲨鱼皮效应或熔体断裂）与模口流动条件相关（参见第5章）。螺杆频率下不稳定性的主要成因在于螺杆泵送段的横向压力梯度。此外，当螺杆螺纹经过进料口时，料斗中聚合物的周期性中断还会额外引发微小压力变化。根据Fenner、Cox和Isherwood（1979）的研究，固体床在加速过程中发生破碎，导致压力在低于螺杆转速5至10倍的频率下剧烈波动。此外，固体床破碎会产生温度分布不均的异质挤出物（Sakai，1990）。固态床中困住的空气逸出以及固态床表面的不平整，也是熔体流动不稳定的重要来源（Becker等人，1990；1991）。后者改变了熔体流动可用空间的有效流体动力学尺寸。当压缩区锥度导致固体床在螺杆通道内重塑形态时，同样会引发熔融过程中断而产生不稳定现象（Bigg, 1987）。压力或流速波动亦可能由温度波动引起，其根源在于温度控制系统性能，或在极低频率下源于环境温度变化。Gitschner和Lutterback（1984）将料筒加热系统启停阶段引发的压力波动与产量波动建立了关联。最后，随机性不稳定通常与前述不规则进料条件相关。同样地，熔融起始位置的任何变化都会影响压力建立曲线（Bigg, 1987）。

Cheng（1990）研究了颗粒几何形状对挤出速率和混合质量的影响。由于颗粒形状既取决于造粒系统，也受操作条件制约，这对复合材料生产商至关重要。冷切系统（如条状切粒）通常能产生比热面切系统（如气水造粒机）更均匀的颗粒几何形状。颗粒形态会随冷却方式、切割速度、模孔几何结构及材料类型而变化。研究发现颗粒尺寸对挤出速率的影响随螺杆转速增加而增强，产量波动幅度可达16%。聚乙烯颗粒（压缩性更高）对颗粒尺寸的依赖性低于刚性更强的聚丙烯颗粒。相对于螺杆槽深的小颗粒具有更高的固体输送率和熔化速率，但其对产量的影响同样取决于螺杆设计。

# 7.2 固体输送与熔融的现象学描述

自20世纪60年代以来，为全面理解聚合物挤出过程，研究人员开展了大量工作。数百篇论文与多部专著相继问世，目前已有若干用于模拟该过程的软件包可供使用。早期理论描述仅限于固体输送与熔体输送，因其可基于连续介质力学原理展开研究。然而要理解熔化机制并描述塑化过程，则需开发特定实验方法。马多克（1959）与斯特里特（1961）创立的“瞬态成像”技术，为挤出研究开辟了新路径。当特定材料挤出达到稳态时，中断螺杆旋转，冷却系统启动并取出螺杆。建议选用配备高效冷却系统的挤出机（带循环冷却介质的料筒）。在原料颗粒中混入部分彩色颗粒，熔融后形成的显著纹理可对应材料沿螺杆运动的轨迹。根据聚合物刚度，材料螺旋结构会被解卷或破坏，并在其长度方向的若干已知位置进行横向切割，以获取工艺的全局视图。

通过仔细观察多种热塑性塑料在不同挤出机中的挤出样本，得出关于该工艺特征的若干普遍结论：

1. 通常可识别出三个功能区，对应材料的不同物理相态。最初所有材料保持固态；随后固态与熔融态共存；最终整个聚合物呈现熔融状态。这些区域通常分别位于螺杆的进料区、压缩区和计量区。但功能区与几何区并无直接对应关系，因为首次熔融位置及熔融速率取决于加工条件、螺杆与模头几何结构以及聚合物的热物理特性。

2. 在螺杆初始旋转阶段，聚合物颗粒处于松散状态（并在螺杆抽吸过程中损失），随着在通道中推进逐渐被压缩，呈现出类似弹性固体的特性。该区域可明显划分为两个子区域： 第一子区属于真正的固体输送阶段（即仅能检测到固态物料），而第二子区在固体/内筒壁顶部的界面处形成薄熔融膜。该区域被称为延迟区，因为尽管已形成熔体，但主要熔融机制尚未启动（Kacir and Tadmor, 1972）。类似的熔融薄膜也可能出现在螺杆螺距和根部附近。

3. 图7.2展示了熔融区螺杆通道的典型（虽理想化）横截面。固体物料与熔融物料完全分离：前者形成高度密实的块状堆积，后者则形成覆盖整个通道高度的熔池。图中熔融仅发生于内筒壁，但实际操作中螺杆根部及尾部螺纹处亦可能（以较低速率）发生熔融。隔离固体与筒体的薄膜厚度随熔池方向逐渐增厚，此现象源于螺杆与筒体间的相对速度，该速度将熔体向螺杆推料螺纹方向挤压。由彩色颗粒与原始聚合物混合形成的痕迹表明，熔池中的材料遵循螺旋路径运动，类似于图6.8(b)和6.8(c)所示。

4. 随着熔化进程推进，熔池宽度增加而固体床宽度减小。固体颗粒填充通道间隙，仅在与金属壁的界面处保留熔融聚合物薄膜。固体颗粒逐渐变形并被压缩。固体床常会过早断裂，其碎片被熔融材料包围。

5. 最终所有材料完全熔化。在熔体输送区，流体元素沿着先前熔池中识别的螺旋轨迹被推动前进。

固体与周围金属表面之间产生的作用力，使其在内筒壁传热效率不断提高的作用下逐渐致密化并升温。经过数圈螺杆旋转后，固体开始表现出塞子特性——即通道内任意点的速度仅沿通道下行方向（z轴）变化。最终，靠近筒壁的材料达到熔点，由此形成延迟区。该点的精确位置取决于热传导机制（与料筒温度相关）与机械能转化为热能机制（取决于摩擦力）的相对强度。固体与内筒壁顶部界面处形成薄熔融膜层。由于粘性耗散作用，该膜层在高剪切速率作用下厚度急剧增加。然而剪切速率的相应渐减将减缓该过程。随后，熔融聚合物薄膜亦可能在螺杆通道的三面交界处形成。此时摩擦作用完全被粘性阻力机制取代，固体与筒体的相对运动将界面薄膜推向螺杆工作螺纹。若薄膜厚度小于螺杆尖端与内筒壁间的机械间隙，薄膜将流经间隙而不形成熔融池。图7.2所示的熔融机制仅在大部分熔体无法跨越间隙、被迫聚集于螺杆推料螺纹附近时才会启动。这意味着薄膜厚度至少需等于机械间隙值。实践观察表明，当薄膜厚度达到间隙值的5至7倍时熔融区才开始形成。

熔融过程中，螺杆推料螺纹刮除部分熔融薄膜并沉积于熔池。每个流体单元将形成规则且特征鲜明的螺旋纹理，其形态取决于运动启动时单元在通道中的坐标位置——这与先前研究熔体输送时观察到的现象一致。熔融池推动固体床抵靠螺杆尾部螺纹。熔融过程发生于熔融薄膜与固体床的界面。因此，当薄膜被筒体表面拖曳至熔融池时，固体颗粒被迫持续重排，此时料柱获得沿$y$方向的向上速度分量$V_{\mathrm{py}}$，使更多材料暴露于熔融环境。新熔融物随即被带回，暴露更多固体并延续该机制。因此$V_{\mathrm{py}}$可视为熔融速率的度量。该机制通过实验得到验证（Covas和Gilbert，1992）：利用降解聚氯乙烯在紫外线照射下于可见光区域荧光发光的特性，其荧光强度（FI）与降解程度成正比。图7.4展示了两种操作条件下固体床、熔融池及其界面沿通道方向的荧光强度变化。总体而言，荧光强度随停留时间增加而上升。固体床呈现最高荧光强度，因固体颗粒间无相对运动时，配方中稳定剂的效能受限。界面处观测到最低荧光强度值，此处

通常比恒定深度通道缩减更缓慢，甚至可能观察到固相床宽度增加。与此同时，熔体因受限于较小的熔池尺寸而加速流动，导致更多热量产生，即熔体温度升高。该现象对需采用小压缩比螺杆的热敏性聚合物尤为关键。若压缩区过短且深度变化过快，固体暴露熔化的速率可能超过熔化速率。固体将填满通道大部分截面，阻碍熔体池向下流动。由此形成循环过程：在螺杆转速恒定时，产量下降会加速熔化速率，进而促使通道疏通。产量恢复至先前水平后，通道很快再次堵塞。这种剧烈波动严重影响产品均匀性和质量（Klein, 1972）。Rauwendaal（1990）基于操作变量与材料特性量化了避免堵塞的最大压缩比和锥度，并指出采用大压缩比和短压缩区尤为危险。

单螺杆挤出塑化过程中，聚合物颗粒在固体输送区被置于不同螺杆通道坐标时，便形成了独特的加工轨迹。任何靠近内筒壁的颗粒会迅速熔化并融入熔体池，此时流动模式配合较长的停留时间，可能形成可接受的分布式混合。然而，位于螺杆根部附近的颗粒将承受渐进的热量与压力，在接近固体/熔体膜界面时发生显著变形。当颗粒最终熔化时，整体熔化机制可能已近完成，而新熔体在螺杆中的停留时间往往不足以确保与邻近物料充分混合。这种工艺特性在加工热敏性聚合物时尤为不利。如前文关于PVC复合物的讨论及图7.4所示，当物料仍处于固态时，相关添加剂（如热稳定剂）无法渗透颗粒内部延缓降解速率。结果不仅导致物料沿螺杆长度方向平均降解程度加剧，最终固体床也可能发生显著降解。相反，在反向啮合双螺杆挤出机中，由于平均剪切水平较低且固体几乎同时熔融，物料一旦熔化，沿螺杆方向的降解程度便不会增加（Covas and Gilbert, 1988）。

Lindt（1981）将上述机制归因于促进熔体在通道横向流动的条件。这种流动取决于聚合物横向循环与间隙泄漏流之间的相互作用。随着塔德莫尔、德克尔和门格斯-克伦克机制的依次考量，间隙流体压力分量对循环模式的影响愈发显著：塔德莫尔机制以固体床周围的闭合循环为主导，而门格斯-克伦克机制则由机械间隙中的流动决定。Lindt（1976）还指出，随着通道下段压力梯度增大，推动固体床抵靠螺杆推料翼的横向力会减弱。因此对于产生显著压力梯度的高粘度物料，横向力可能反转方向，从而促进Menges-Klenk机制的发生。

# 7.3 理论分析

相较于其他功能区域，固体输送环节长期未受重视。固体输送通常仅是塑化过程中的次要环节，
发生在螺杆前几圈运转阶段，极少影响设备产量。多数分析仍遵循固体输送理论先驱Darnell与Mol（1953）的核心假设——即固体颗粒被压缩成固体塞，在筒体与螺杆表面承受摩擦力。该模型虽经逐步改进——详见芬纳（1977）及阿梅拉尔、拉弗勒与阿尔潘（1990）的综述——但基本框架基本未变。近期方、陈与朱（1991）计算了螺杆初始转动阶段（固体塞形成前）颗粒的三维速度与应力分布。松散颗粒的剪切阻力微弱，在微小外力作用下即可流动。Chung（1970）采用不同方法处理该问题，假设弹性固体床通过熔体塞表面摩擦产生的聚合物熔体薄膜所形成的粘性阻力机制向前输送。

塔德莫尔（Tadmor and Klein, 1970）提出的定量熔融理论为后续调整与改进奠定了基础。该理论最初假设：在恒定下行速度移动的刚性固体床顶部，会形成厚度恒定的熔融薄膜覆盖整个通道。后期研究同时考虑了螺杆表面附近熔融薄膜的存在，以及刚性或可变形固体床（即包含自由变形颗粒或无凝聚力的固体团聚体）。在最精密的模型中，通道横截面被划分为多达五个独立区域，固体或熔体的行为通过连续介质平衡方程描述。关于熔融过程建模的综述文献已有多篇发表（Fenner, 1977; Lindt, 1985; Amellal, Lafleur and Arpin, 1990）。Lindt（1985）指出所有模型均能合理估算熔融沿螺杆长度的推进过程，但熔融区压力变化对各模型假设的敏感度存在显著差异。可变形床模型严重低估压力值，而刚性床模型则能获得更优结果。因此Lindt建议采用非牛顿流体分析法研究熔体流动模式（特别是横向通道循环），并结合刚性固体床概念。这要求模型除熔池和上部熔融薄膜外，还需纳入与螺杆表面接触的下部薄膜。该课题持续吸引着研究者的关注。Vincelette等人（1989）修改了Tadmor模型，通过固床加速度参数（SBAP）——该参数由Donovan（1971）定义——计算熔化速率，该参数反映固床速度的变化。李与韩（1990）对林特团队提出的非等温非牛顿五区模型（Elbirli等，1984；Lindt与Elbirli，1985）进行改进，基于固床表面受力平衡原理及固床体积状态弹性模量，引入固床变形概念。该模型后经实验验证（韩、李与惠勒，1990）。学界亦尝试寻求解析解（参见波坦特，1991）。例如劳文达尔（1991-92）推导出

幂律流体熔解的精确解析解。显然，现有理论尚无法预测床层过早破裂及其后续效应。通过引入聚合物的粘弹性特性改进现有熔解模型，或开展更全面的固体床力学研究（以实现过早破裂预测），无疑具有重要意义。但同时需权衡计算要求与成本，评估其对预测精度的实际效益。


# 7.3.1 固体输送

# 输送机制

本节采用塔德莫尔与克莱因（1970）基于达内尔与莫尔（1953）框架提出的分析模型。尽管该模型结构简洁，却为理解和优化输送过程提供了便捷工具。同时考虑了塔德莫尔及其合作者（布罗耶与塔德莫尔，1972；卡西尔与塔德莫尔，1972；塔德莫尔与布罗耶，1972）提出的部分改进方案。

固体床在料斗表面与固体床顶部间的切向摩擦力作用下，沿下料方向被拖曳移动。该运动受到作用于螺杆根部和螺旋叶片上的摩擦力阻滞。因此固体床获得沿槽道下行方向的速度 $V_{\mathrm{pz}}$。固体沿槽道方向压力递增，但该压力梯度与物料运动方向相反。本文作出以下假设：

1. 固体行为视为弹性连续体；固体内部无变形，呈恒定速度的塞流状态。

2. 料道内聚合物完全充满。此条件确保塞流及物料压实，从而产生压力。实际操作中，当采用计量式断料进料模式，或料斗/挤出机进料口发生流体中断时，该假设不成立。

3. 料道深度均匀。但若按小步长计算料道深度变化，可考虑深度变化因素。

4. 料筒与螺杆间的机械间隙不存在逆流现象。

5. 聚合物密度与摩擦系数与压力温度无关。此假设存疑，但可通过采用上述方法规避。

6. 应力分布各向同性，因此压力仅随通道纵向距离变化。

7. 材料处于恒定均匀温度。同样可通过微小$\Delta z$增量处理料筒温度变化。

部分假设已被不同学者放宽。通道采用直角坐标系描述，即假设通道从螺杆展开后形成截面为矩形的完美线性管道。通道深度 $h$ 与螺杆外径 $D$ 的比值越小，此简化引入的误差越小。将螺杆视为静止物体，将料筒视为在通道顶部以恒定速度 $V_{\mathrm{b}}$ 滑动的无限板（其运动方向与通道下行方向夹角 $\phi$，即螺旋角），这种简化更易于分析。虽然该简化改变了作用于聚合物的惯性力效应，但产生的误差较小。

图7.10展示了纵向剖面视图下固体塞的速度分量。固体沿通道以速度 $V_{\mathrm{pz}}$ 运动，其切向分量与轴向分量分别为 $V_{\mathrm{p}\phi}$  and  $V_{\mathrm{pa}}$ 。由于固体输出量等于其速度与有效截面积的乘积，可写为：


$$
Q _ {\mathrm {p}} = V _ {\mathrm {p a}} \left[ \pi \left(D ^ {2} - D _ {\mathrm {i}} ^ {2}\right) / 4 - i t h / \sin \bar {\phi} \right] \tag {7.2}
$$


其中 $D_{\mathrm{i}}$ 为螺钉内径，$i$ 为螺纹圈数，$t$ 为螺距。下标 $s$ 和 $b$ 分别表示在螺钉根部和内筒壁处测得的数值。项 $i t h / \sin \bar {\phi}$  表示螺纹投影到与螺杆轴线垂直平面上的面积。如上所述，固体输送机构源于金属接触面产生的摩擦力差。

$$
\tan \theta = \frac {V _ {\mathrm {p a}}}{V _ {\mathrm {b}} - V _ {\mathrm {p} \phi}} = \frac {V _ {\mathrm {p a}}}{V _ {\mathrm {b}} - V _ {\mathrm {p a}} / \tan \phi_ {\mathrm {b}}} \tag {7.3}
$$

因此,

$$
V _ {\mathrm {p a}} = V _ {\mathrm {b}} \frac {\tan \theta \tan \phi_ {\mathrm {b}}}{\tan \theta + \tan \phi_ {\mathrm {b}}} \tag {7.4}
$$

# Flow in hoppers

As seen above, evaluating the efficiency of solids conveying requires information on  $P_{1}$ , i.e. on the initial pressure at the beginning of the process. Usually, one assumes that this pressure equals that at the bottom of the hopper, which can be computed using bulk solids analysis. However, this approach ignores the complex transition from the gravitational flow of particulate solids in the hopper to the drag induced flow in the screw channel. The situation is further complicated during the changeover from static storage

to flow mode, as an overpressure propagates rapidly upwards through the material (Shamlou, 1988). Nevertheless, the method provides a link between the performances of the hopper and of the extruder, thus reflecting the practical experience of such a dependence. It is also possible to ignore the hopper, assuming instead that  $P_{1}$  results from the effect of local gravitational and centrifugal forces (Lovegrove and Williams, 1973). This approach accounts for the possibility of the channel working partially full of material before the development of plug flow. However, it fails to consider the already mentioned connection between the hopper and the extruder. As Tadmor and Gogos (1979) have pointed out, there is a clear need for a model which includes the hopper, the inlet region of the extruder and the first screw turns (where the Darnell-Mol model cannot be applied).

Under these circumstances, the purpose of modelling the flow of material in the hopper is: to ensure an adequate throughput, i.e. that the productivity of the process is not limited by the hopper; to provide a link between the hopper and the extruder (via the parameter  $P_{1}$ ); and to establish the loading/operating conditions that minimize pressure fluctuations at the bottom of the vessel.

The geometry of the hopper dictates the flow pattern and the resulting pressure profile developed within the solids during discharge. Hoppers can be conical, where an axisymmetric flow develops, or wedge-shaped, in which plane flow occurs. The half-angle is approximately  $8 - 10^{\circ}$  larger in this case, providing more effective storage capacity, but this is offset by the eventual build-up that is likely to take place in the sharp corners (Roberts, 1991). Two distinct flow patterns may develop. During mass flow there is a bulk flow of granules towards the exit, with only a small velocity gradient, guaranteeing the complete discharge of the vessel. In funnel flow the movement is confined to a vertical region in the centre of the vessel. A stable pipe is formed, with the stagnant material acting as a support. Flow is erratic and may cause segregation. Combinations of the two situations are also possible. Mass discharge is preferable, since flow usually initiates as soon as the outlet is opened, there is little tendency for flow disruptions (such as arching), and dead zones do not appear, with the first material going in being the first going out.

Currently, there are no theories capable of correlating the influence of the discharge rate upon the vertical pressure profile. Only situations of static loading or open discharge are usually considered. The derivation of equations quantifying the flow rate is complicated by the complex, and still little understood, rheology of solids in granular or powder form. Consequently, most authors fit correlations to their empirical data, or adopt dimensional analyses. Equations such as the one proposed by Beverloo, Leniger and de Velde (1961) quantify the gravity discharge of coarse particles through flat or converging vessels. For central conical apertures, the rate of mass discharge is given by:

$$
Q = 0. 5 8 \rho g ^ {0. 5} (D - k d _ {\mathrm {p}}) ^ {2. 5} \tag {7.23}
$$

where  $D$  is the aperture diameter,  $d_{\mathrm{p}}$  is the diameter of the grains and  $k$  is a particle shape constant, with an average value of 1.4. Considering a conical hopper with a  $6\mathrm{cm}$  opening, containing pellets approximately  $3\mathrm{mm}$  in diameter and a material with a bulk density of  $600\mathrm{kg}\mathrm{m}^{-3}$ , the mass flow rate is predicted to reach  $3000\mathrm{kg}\mathrm{h}^{-1}$ . This is more than one order of magnitude above the expected throughput of a conventional extruder having the same feed port geometry. Thus, the pressure profile of the solids in the hopper of an extruder is probably more adequately described assuming static conditions, whereas for designing purposes discharge conditions should be considered (in order to account for the significant overpressures developed). There is now a more significant dependence of the base pressure on the loading conditions.

A century ago, Janssen (1895) considered the equilibrium forces acting on a small material slice contained in a vertical cylindrical column of solids, and predicted the vertical pressure profile:

$$
P _ {\mathrm {v}} = \rho g D \left\{1 - \exp \left[ - (K f D / 4) h \right] \right\} / 4 f K \tag {7.24}
$$

where  $\rho$  represents the material bulk density,  $g$  acceleration due to gravity,  $f$  the coefficient of wall friction,  $D$  the hopper diameter,  $h$  the depth from the top surface and  $K$  the ratio of lateral to normal pressure in the vessel. The weight of the solids is largely sustained by the lateral walls. Whereas at the top of the solids ( $h = 0$ )  $P_{\mathrm{v}}$  matches the hydrostatic pressure,  $h \to \infty$  produces a maximum value:

$$
P _ {\mathrm {v}} (\max) = \rho g D / 4 f \tag {7.25}
$$

Thus, unlike a column of liquids where the hydrostatic pressure at the bottom increases linearly with the height, in a column of solids the bulk pressure increases exponentially until an asymptotic value is reached. The location of this critical height depends both on the geometry and on the material properties. For practical purposes, the critical height corresponds to the minimum level of solids to be preserved in the hopper, if variations in the pressure in the initial turns of the screw are to be avoided. For the convergent region (static conditions), Walker (1966) postulated the inexistence of shear stresses on vertical planes and assumed that the vertical pressure at any level is equivalent to the hydrostatic pressure due to the amount of material above it. The resulting linear vertical pressure distribution is computed from:

$$
P _ {\mathrm {v}} = \rho g \left[ \left(P _ {\mathrm {v o}} / \rho g\right) + h \right] \tag {7.26}
$$

$P_{\mathrm{vo}}$  expresses the pressure at the top of the convergence and  $h$  the height considered. # The delay zone

When, due to the combined effects of the heat conducted and generated, the solid near the barrel wall reaches its melting point, a thin melt film is formed. From this location onwards, the solids conveying mechanism, based on the differences in friction forces acting at the solid interfaces, is replaced by another using drag flow, the role of friction now being played by the polymer viscosity. The delay zone evolves until the conditions for initiating the fusion mechanism are created. In principle, it would be anticipated that it progresses until the melted film thickness equals the mechanical gap between the flight of the screw and the inner barrel wall. Then, as the throughput capacity of the gap became lower than the amount of leakage flow of melted material, a melt pool would form near the pushing flight of the screw. In practice, however, it has been observed that the melt pool only grows when the film thickness reaches 5-7 times the value of the gap. This behaviour can result from several possible causes (Agassant, Aynes and Sergent, 1986):

1. The backward film flow drags the solid plug against the pushing flight of the screw, thus making the process initiation difficult.

2. The melt pool can only appear when the solid bed deforms, i.e. when the local pressure is larger than the bed yield stress. The maximum pressure drop in the gap, in the cross-channel direction, occurs when the film thickness reaches three times the mechanical gap. It has been shown (Kacir and Tadmor, 1972; Agassant, Avenas and Sergent, 1986) that, for an isothermal Newtonian melt, the cross-channel pressure is given by:

$$
\Delta P = \frac {6 \eta V _ {\mathrm {b}}}{\sin \phi_ {\mathrm {b}}} \frac {\delta_ {\mathrm {f}} - \delta}{\frac {\delta^ {3}}{t} + \frac {\delta_ {\mathrm {f}} ^ {3}}{b}} \tag {7.27}
$$

where  $\eta$  is the polymer viscosity, and  $\delta_{\mathrm{f}}$  and  $\delta$  the thicknesses of the film and of the gap, respectively. The pressure increases with increasing difference between  $\delta_{\mathrm{f}}$  and  $\delta$ , passing through a maximum when the film thickness equals 1.5 times the flight clearance. If non-Newtonian and non-isothermal effects are taken into account, the maximum is now attained when  $\delta_{\mathrm{f}}$  is about 2.5–3 times the flight clearance, as mentioned above. If the gap is sufficiently high, the stresses created will be lower than the material's yield stress. In this case the Tadmor melting mechanism will not occur, or will develop very slowly. Instead, a mechanism similar to Dekker's may evolve.

3. The melted material can eventually penetrate within the intergranular spaces, the film growing in thickness at a lower pace.

The growth in the film thickness is directly dependent upon the relative contribution of heat conducted and generated. While the heat transfer mechanism is similar to that taking place in the melting zone, in the delay zone any newly melted material accumulates in the film, increasing its

thickness, instead of being scraped by the screw and deposited in the melt pool. However, the model does not hold for the initial stages of film formation, where the problem of melting solid polymer particles has to be solved. Several authors have studied the melting of a granular solid bed (Pearson, 1976; Kuo and Chung, 1989), albeit assuming the initial presence of a thin melt film. Kacir and Tadmor (1972) tackled the problem assuming spherical polymer particles and melt penetration in the gaps no deeper than half the particle diameter. The results showed that only  $5\%$  of the delay zone should be treated by this model.

It is not easy to predict the location of the transition from the delay to the adjacent melting zone. The two most popular criteria for determining the length of the delay zone are: to compute the length of the channel needed to develop a film of material with about 5.6 times the thickness of the mechanical gap, which was found experimentally to be the average film thickness at the end of the delay zone (Kacir and Tadmor, 1972); and to adopt empirical correlations based upon the results of visualization experiments using different thermoplastics. Figure 7.21 shows a relationship between the length of the delay zone and the rate of fusion, expressed by a dimensionless melt parameter  $\psi$  (Tadmor and Klein, 1970).

In this zone, pressure initially develops due to viscous drag on the barrel and friction on the screw. Thus, not only does equation (7.6) remain valid, but also in the force balance on the solid plug shown in Fig. 7.11 one simply has to replace the frictional force  $F_{1}$  (equation (7.7) with a viscous drag force given by:

$$
F _ {1} = \tau b _ {\mathrm {b}} \mathrm {d} z _ {\mathrm {b}}, \tag {7.28}
$$

where  $\tau$  is the shear stress. The average shear rate in the film is:

$$
\dot {\gamma} = \frac {V _ {\mathrm {b}} \sin \phi_ {\mathrm {b}}}{\delta_ {\mathrm {f}} \sin (\phi_ {\mathrm {b}} + \theta)} \tag {7.29}
$$

Figure 7.21 Length of the delay zone, in terms of a melting parameter  $\psi$  (adapted from Tadmor and Klein, 1970).


The resulting pressure rise is (Kacir and Tadmor, 1972):

$$
\begin{array}{l} P _ {3} = \frac {\tau b _ {\mathrm {b}} (\cos \theta - K \sin \theta)}{A _ {3} K - B _ {3}} \left[ 1 - \exp \left(\frac {B _ {3} - A _ {3} K}{A _ {2} K + B _ {2}} Z _ {\mathrm {b}}\right) \right] \\ + P _ {2} \exp \left(\frac {B _ {3} - A _ {3} K}{A _ {2} K + B _ {2}} Z _ {\mathrm {b}}\right) \tag {7.30} \\ \end{array}
$$

where  $A$  and  $B$  are, as before (see equation (7.17)), constants depending on the system geometry, on the friction coefficients and on the solids conveying angle. The driving force  $F_{1}$  is high in the preliminary stages of the process, because significant shear stresses are generated by the small film thicknesses (see equation (7.29)). As the film grows in thickness, the shear rate decreases and  $F_{1}$  declines. Thus, it is possible to observe a pressure rise at the beginning of the delay zone, followed by a drop. The situation may be more complicated because, although the majority of melting occurs near the inner barrel wall, the friction coefficients at the other solid bed surfaces may also induce the melting of the material. This condition is analogous to that assumed by Chung (1970) who studied the conveying mechanism of the solid plug surrounded by a film of melted material. Although equation (7.6) still holds, when the thickness of the various interfacial films is identical and the material follows the power law, a linear pressure rise along the channel is predicted.

# Grooved feed bushes

As shown previously (and illustrated in Fig. 7.12), the efficiency and stability of solids conveying increases with an increasing difference between the coefficients of friction between polymer and barrel and between polymer and screw. Figure 7.18 extended this concept to plasticating extrusion, demonstrating, for a case study, that the effective process throughput improves as the ratio  $f_{\mathrm{b}} / f_{\mathrm{s}}$  increases. One practical way of ensuring this mode of operation consists in a dramatic increase in the inner barrel wall surface roughness via the machining of either axial or helicoidal grooves. This solution was initially proposed and tested in Germany in the early 1970s, for processing high molecular weight HDPE, and has become popular throughout Europe. Machines with a grooved feed bush are often claimed to be positive pumping devices, with the output directly proportional to the material density and screw speed, and independent of the die resistance (however, despite this positive feeding and early good pressure generation, the screw still has to melt and adequately homogenize the material which, in itself, often limits the rate of material that can be fed).

Practical experience has proved that the number and geometry of the grooves depends on the screw diameter, on the nature of the polymer being processed and on the type of feedstock. Usually, the number of grooves increases with the screw diameter and upon processing softer plastics. The total

number of grooves is usually between  $D / 10$  and  $D / 5$ . Frequently, the grooves have a rectangular cross-section, their depth decreasing linearly, from a maximum below the hopper opening, through a length of  $2D$  to  $4D$ . Lower angles of taper compress the material less, thus inducing lower shear stresses. Although the main purpose of the grooves is to increase the equivalent frictional coefficient, their considerable dimensions relative to the channel depth and pellet size often modify the conventional flow of solids. Grünschloss (1990) observed the flow of powders in a tapered feed bush using a video technique, and concluded that in axial grooves there is a considerable velocity of solids both along and across the slots, whereas in the helicoidal solution the material moves along the small channels, thus improving self-cleaning. The velocity fields affect the pressure generation mechanism and make the mathematical treatment of this zone quite complex. Also, they probably justify the relatively modest increase in the performance of grooved feed extruders when processing powders.

Machining grooves in the barrel significantly alters the characteristics of an extruder and requires several modifications to its design and construction.

- A capacity for cooling the grooved bush, and existence of a thermal barrier in the remaining length of the barrel. It is necessary to avoid the premature melting of the material due to the intense heat created by the high friction forces (the rate of heat generation at the barrel wall is given in equation (7.20), and is proportional to the pressure generated and to  $f_{\mathrm{b}}$ ). The energy lost through the cooling channels may reach 15–40% of the mechanical power input, depending on the materials being processed. However, the 'specific power consumption' (i.e. the energy consumption per unit of extruded product) may be lower than in conventional machines, as the increase in energy required is more than compensated by the increased transport capability (Rauwendaal, 1990). Based on the fact that the friction coefficients of plastics generally increase with increasing temperature, Menges, Feistkorn and Fischbach (1984) postulated that the grooved bush must not cool the solids too fast, but should only prevent the formation of a melt film. Using a temperature-controlled grooved bush (with the water kept at  $70^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ ), they observed higher melt throughputs and claimed an increase in the efficiency from 45% to 80%. Figure 7.22 complements the information presented in Fig. 7.18 and illustrates how controlling the temperature of the initial barrel section influences the plasticating process. The overall effect of the grooves was simulated by considering a relatively high value of  $f_{\mathrm{b}}$ , which was taken as 4.5 times higher than friction at the screw surfaces. When a conventional barrel temperature profile is selected (see the barrel temperature profile in Fig. 7.18), increasing friction at the barrel wall augments the mass throughput from 44.6 to  $51.1\mathrm{kg}\mathrm{h}^{-1}$ . If the barrel temperature is kept at  $70^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  for  $0.18\mathrm{m}$  (equivalent to  $3D$ ), the corresponding lengthening of the

In particular, for high-friction barrel walls, the output rises from 51.1 to  $64.5\mathrm{kg}\mathrm{h}^{-1}$ . Pressures reaching  $100\mathrm{MPa}$  are predicted, and are often observed in practice.

- Use of stronger materials for screws and barrels. Increasing friction near the barrel wall promotes wear and produces a significant early pressure development that can reach  $100 - 300\mathrm{MPa}$ . Thus, not only does the length of the grooves have to be kept short (usually less than  $5D$ ), but also the materials used for construction have to be strong and wear resistant.

- Motor with increased power. As equation (7.21) indicates, increasing  $f_{\mathrm{b}}$  requires a bigger torque at the same screw speed.

- Screws with lower compression ratios. With the aim of maintaining the pressures within reasonable limits, screws with a low compression ratio are usually adopted. But, since higher throughputs imply smaller residence times in the extruder, greater melting efficiency is required. This has resulted in the development of complex screw profiles, fitted with shearing and mixing sections or rings.

The loss of versatility of the extruder caused by the need to modify the screw, the enhanced wear possibilities, the increase in machine cost and greater quality concerns (there is a possibility of melting being overrun by the additional output of solids), have led some authors to conclude that the use of a grooved feed section is not usually justifiable, as good characteristics are usually obtainable with a properly sized extruder, working with a good screw (Steward, 1989). However, continued improvements in the theoretical

understanding and in the technology of grooved feed extruders have resulted in their extensive use in blow moulding, film blowing and pipe and profile extrusion. The higher machine costs and other disadvantages are felt to be of secondary importance compared with the benefits exhibited upon processing.

As seen above, the mathematical treatment of the effect of the grooves is complex (for a list of references on previous theoretical work, see Potente, 1985). Potente (1985) introduced simple approximate solutions to calculate the equivalent coefficient of friction,  $f_{\mathrm{bg}}$ , the friction coefficient at the barrel wall resulting from the effect of the geometry of the grooves. The following equation provided good agreement with the experimental data:

$$
f _ {\mathrm {b g}} = f _ {\mathrm {b}} + \left(f ^ {*} - f _ {\mathrm {b}}\right) \frac {b _ {\mathrm {g}} N _ {\mathrm {g}}}{\pi D} \left\{1 - \exp \left[ - 5 \left(\frac {h _ {\mathrm {g}} N _ {\mathrm {g}}}{b _ {\mathrm {g}}}\right) ^ {0. 9} \right] \right\} \tag {7.31}
$$

where  $f^{*}$  is the internal friction coefficient,  $b_{\mathrm{g}}$  groove width,  $N_{\mathrm{g}}$  the number of grooves and  $h_{\mathrm{g}}$  the maximum groove depth.

It is also possible to take into account the differences in the groove base and barrel surface roughnesses. The advantages of helicoidal grooves have been quantitatively explained by Rauwendaal (1990). The optimum helix angle increases with increasing screw helix angle and decreasing groove depth.

# 7.3.2 Melting

The model developed by Tadmor and Klein (1970) provides the means to understand and describe the Maddock melting mechanism developed in single-screw extruders with reasonable mathematical complexity. The resulting analytical equations can readily be used to study the effect of the system geometry, of the operating conditions, or of the thermophysical properties of the polymer on the melting rate. The model is quite popular in the literature and is frequently used in extrusion software packages. It will also be adopted in this section, providing coherent physical boundary conditions with the adjacent functional zones.

Consider Fig. 7.23, which represents a section of the screw channel during the development of the Tadmor melting mechanism (the figure corresponds essentially to one of the cuts considered in Fig. 7.3 and is a schematization of the cross-section depicted in Fig. 7.2). As before, a rectangular coordinate system was adopted, and the barrel was taken as an infinite plate sliding at constant velocity  $V_{\mathrm{b}}$  in a direction making an angle  $\phi$  with the downchannel direction,  $z$ . Both the solid bed and the melt pool have rectangular cross-sections, the melt pool encompassing the height of the channel, whereas a thin melt film, with thickness  $\delta_{\mathrm{f}}$ , separates the solid bed from the inner barrel wall. While the barrel is kept at  $T_{\mathrm{b}}$ , the solid bed/melt film interface is at the polymer melt temperature,  $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ . At any particular location, the solid occupies a width  $X$ , which is obviously smaller than the channel width,  $b$ . The plug moves at

Thus, fusion of the solids takes place at the top surface of the plug, the resulting film of freshly melted material being dragged in the  $xx$  direction towards the melt pool. The melting mechanism is initiated when  $X = b$  and will be completed when  $X = 0$ . It is important to know the progression of melting along the screw and to determine the length of the channel, usually denoted by  $z_{\mathrm{t}}$ , occupied in melting the material, since it establishes the remaining portion of the screw available for the subsequent melt pumping and mixing stages. The following conditions are assumed:

- The channel is rectangular, with width  $b$  and depth  $h$ , and runs full of polymer. The influence of the gap in terms of the melting progression and output is considered to be negligible.

- The solid bed behaves as a homogeneous elastic continuum.

- A steady state exists. At any location of the channel in the melting zone the barrel is at temperature  $T_{\mathrm{b}}$  and the melt film/solid bed interface is at  $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ . The solid occupies the width  $X$ , whose value decreases in the downchannel direction.

- The solid bed has infinite depth in terms of heat transfer. Heat transfer from or to the screw is neglected. This simplification ignores the progressive heating of the solid bed as it moves along the screw and precludes the model predicting the usual acceleration of melting during the later stages of the process. Thus, the predictions are frequently conservative.

- Melting occurs only at the interface defined by the outer surface of the solid bed and the film near the inner barrel wall. Since conduction from the melt pool is ignored, the problem is reduced to one-dimensional heat transfer. Although most of the melting occurs in the interface considered, the predictions will again become conservative. Nevertheless, avoiding this simplification would complicate the computations considerably, as a four-or a five-zone model would have to be adopted to take into account the presence of the films surrounding the solids.

- The melted film moves between parallel plates. The solid bed/melt film interface is assumed to be sharp and flat, thus simplifying the more realistic geometry shown in Fig. 7.2. The flow develops between two parallel plates moving at unequal speeds,  $V_{\mathrm{b}}$  and  $V_{\mathrm{pz}}$ , and in different directions. The width of solids  $X$ , at any channel location can be determined through a mass balance in the solids. Considering a small plug increment in the downchannel direction, the rate of solids entering the element in the location  $\Delta z$  equals the sum of the rate of solids leaving the element at a location  $(z + \Delta z)$  and through the solids/melt film interface, due to fusion:

\rho_ {\mathrm {p}} V _ {\mathrm {p} z} h X \mid_ {z} = \rho_ {\mathrm {p}} V _ {\mathrm {p} z} h X \mid_ {z + \Delta z} + V _ {\mathrm {p} y} \rho_ {\mathrm {p}} \bar {X} \Delta z \tag {7.35}

where  $G$  is the mass flow rate of the plasticating process (which is assumed to be known) and  $h_0$  is the channel depth at the onset of the melting mechanism. In this case, the rate of heat conducted to the interface by the melted film is partially removed from the interface to the solids by conduction, and partially used to melt the material. Mathematically:

$$

with

\delta_ {\mathrm {f}} = \left\{\frac {\left[ 2 k _ {\mathrm {m}} \left(T _ {\mathrm {b}} - T _ {\mathrm {m}}\right) + \eta V _ {j} ^ {2} \right] X}{V _ {\mathrm {b x}} \rho_ {\mathrm {p}} \left[ C _ {\mathrm {p}} \left(T _ {\mathrm {m}} - T _ {\mathrm {p}}\right) + \lambda \right]} \right\} ^ {2} \tag {7.44}
$$

$$
\Omega = \sqrt {\left\{\frac {V _ {\mathrm {b x}} \rho_ {\mathrm {m}} \left[ k _ {\mathrm {m}} \left(T _ {\mathrm {b}} - T _ {\mathrm {m}}\right) + \eta V _ {j} ^ {2} / 2 \right]}{2 \left[ C _ {\mathrm {p}} \left(T _ {\mathrm {m}} - T _ {\mathrm {p}}\right) + \lambda \right]} \right\}} \tag {7.45}
$$

The parameter  $\Omega$  is a measure of the melting rate. The numerator is proportional to the rate of heat being supplied for melting, both from conduction (first term) and from viscous dissipation (second term). The denominator represents the heat required for converting the solid at temperature  $T_{\mathrm{p}}$  into a melt at  $T_{\mathrm{m}}$ . The relevant polymer properties are the heat capacity,  $C_{\mathrm{p}}$ , and the heat of fusion,  $\lambda$ . The melting rate increases with increasing barrel temperature,  $T_{\mathrm{b}}$ , and screw speed (contained in  $V_{\mathrm{bx}}$ ), but this is obviously limited by the eventual polymer degradation, as expressed by the Brinkman number,  $B_{\mathrm{r}}$ . The melting rate also increases by preheating the solid polymer,  $T_{\mathrm{p}}$ , since it decreases the heat required for melting. However, this solution must be considered with caution, as the polymer pellets may stick to the feed opening walls, hindering the flow of solids to the first screw turns.

5. Mass balance in the solid bed. The following differential equation describes the melting process:

$$
- \frac {\mathrm {d} (h X)}{\mathrm {d} z} = \frac {\omega}{\rho_ {\mathrm {p}} V _ {\mathrm {p z}}} \tag {7.46}
$$

6. Influence of the system geometry. For a constant-depth channel, inserting equation (7.43) in (7.46) and integrating, taking as initial condition  $X(z_{1}) = X_{1}$  (where  $z_{1}$  is an arbitrary helical distance), and using equation (7.36):

$$
\frac {X}{b} = \frac {X _ {1}}{b} \left[ 1 - \frac {\Omega b h _ {0}}{2 G h \sqrt {X _ {1}}} (z - z _ {1}) \right] ^ {2} \tag {7.47}
$$

If  $X(0) = b$

$$
\frac {X}{b} = \left(1 - \frac {\Omega \sqrt {b} h _ {0}}{2 G h} z\right) ^ {2} \tag {7.48}
$$

The total helical length required for melting,  $z_{\mathrm{t}}$ , can be determined from equation (7.48), with  $X = 0$  (completion of melting):

$$
z _ {\mathrm {t}} = \frac {2 G h}{\Omega \sqrt {b} h _ {0}}. \tag {7.49}
$$

If melting is completed in the geometrical section in which it started,  $h \simeq h_0$ . As seen in section 7.3.1, and illustrated in Fig. 7.21, the delay zone downchannel length may be correlated with a dimensionless melting parameter,  $\psi$ . This parameter represents the ratio of the melting rate per unit downchannel solid/ melt interface  $(\omega / X)$  to the solids rate  $(V_{\mathrm{pz}} \rho_{\mathrm{p}})$ :

$$
\psi = \frac {\Omega}{V _ {\mathrm {p z}} \rho_ {\mathrm {p}} \sqrt {X} _ {1}} = \frac {\Omega \sqrt {b}}{G / h _ {0}} \tag {7.50}
$$

Equations (7.47)-(7.49) can be rewritten, respectively, as:

$$
\frac {X}{b} = \frac {X _ {1}}{b} \left[ 1 - \frac {\psi}{2 h} (z - z _ {1}) \right] ^ {2} \tag {7.51}
$$

$$
\frac {X}{b} = \left(1 - \frac {\psi}{2 h} z\right) ^ {2} \tag {7.52}
$$

$$
z _ {\mathrm {t}} = \frac {2 h}{\psi} \tag {7.53}
$$

For a tapered channel with a linearly decreasing channel depth:

$$
h = h _ {1} - A z \tag {7.54}
$$

where  $h_1 = h(z = 0)$ , and  $A$  is the degree of tapering (given by  $(h_1 - h_2) / Z_{\mathrm{comp}}$ ), equations (7.51)-(7.53) become, respectively:

$$
\frac {X}{b} = \frac {X _ {1}}{b} \left[ \frac {\psi}{A} - \left(\frac {\psi}{A} - 1\right) \sqrt {\left(\frac {h _ {1}}{h _ {1} - A z}\right)} \right] ^ {2} \tag {7.55}
$$

$$
\frac {X}{b} = \left[ \frac {\psi}{A} - \left(\frac {\psi}{A} - 1\right) \sqrt {\left(\frac {h _ {1}}{h _ {1} - A z}\right)} \right] ^ {2} \tag {7.56}
$$

$$
z _ {\mathrm {t}} = \frac {h _ {1}}{\psi} \left(2 - \frac {A}{\psi}\right) \tag {7.57}
$$

Tadmor and Gogos (1979) included the effect of convection on the temperature profile in the melt film and concluded that the above equations overestimate the melting rate. They also derived relations equivalent to those presented here, but for a power-law fluid.

If the progression of melting along the screw or the channel length required for melting is to be calculated, data on the thermophysical properties of the polymer, on the system geometry and on the mass throughput must be available. The melting rate (equation (7.45)) should first be computed and its value inserted in either equations (7.51)-(7.53) or (7.55)-(7.57), depending on the geometry of the system. However, to determine  $\Omega$ ,  $V_{j}$  (computed from equation (7.32) with (7.36)) and  $\eta$  must be known. Thus, the average film temperature and the shear rate must be estimated. The former may be taken, as a first approximation, and neglecting viscous dissipation, as  $(T_{\mathrm{b}} + T_{\mathrm{m}}) / 2$ . The second is given by the ratio  $V_{j} / \delta_{\mathrm{f}}$ . The average film thickness may be estimated as half of its maximum value, which is reached at the beginning of the melting process, as expressed in equation (7.44). Considering the influence of the viscous dissipation, the average film temperature is:

$$
\bar {T} = \frac {1}{\delta_ {\mathrm {f}}} \int_ {0} ^ {\delta_ {\mathrm {f}}} T \mathrm {d} y = \frac {T + T _ {\mathrm {m}}}{2} + \frac {\eta V _ {j} ^ {2}}{1 2 k _ {\mathrm {m}}} \tag {7.58}
$$

For a constant-depth channel, the melting length is proportional to the mass flow rate and inversely proportional to the melting rate,  $\Omega$  (equation (7.49)). Since the effect of the operating conditions is contained in this parameter (see equation (7.45)), one can easily arrive at three conclusions. First, an increase in the screw speed (contained in  $V_{j}$ ) promotes viscous dissipation, and hence induces higher melting rates. Unfortunately, the conduction term becomes smaller, as the average material residence time inside the extruder diminishes. The overall effect will certainly depend upon the levels of polymer viscosity. Second, increasing barrel temperatures raises the melting rate, because the contribution of conduction increases. The barrel set temperatures are obviously limited by the possibility of the thermal degradation of the polymer. Simultaneously, the viscous dissipation component will drop, due to the progressive reduction of the melt viscosity. According to correlation (7.45), the heat necessary for melting is  $[C_{\mathrm{p}}(T_{\mathrm{m}} - T_{\mathrm{p}}) + \lambda]$ . In fact, heat is required not only to melt the material, but also to bring it to the average temperature of the film,  $\bar{T}$  (equation (7.58)). Thus, the total heat to be input is:

$$
q _ {\mathrm {b}} = C _ {\mathrm {m}} \left(\bar {T} - T _ {\mathrm {m}}\right) + C _ {\mathrm {p}} \left(T _ {\mathrm {m}} - T _ {\mathrm {p}}\right) + \lambda \tag {7.59}
$$

From the above considerations, it follows that for a specific screw speed, material, and extruder geometry, there may be an optimum barrel temperature maximizing the joint influence of heat conduction and viscous dissipation. The mechanical power supplied by the screw drive during melting may be calculated as the rate of working at the interface between the polymer and the inner barrel wall (in the melting zone). Therefore, the total power consumption results from the contribution of the power used up in the melt film,  $E_{\mathrm{f}}$ , in the melt pool,  $E_{\mathrm{m}}$ , and in the mechanical gap,  $E_{\mathrm{g}}$ :

$$
E _ {\mathrm {w}} = E _ {\mathrm {f}} + E _ {\mathrm {m}} + E _ {\mathrm {g}} \tag {7.62}
$$

Each component may be computed by integrating the product of the velocity and force (shear stress) over the relevant barrel surface. For the Newtonian isothermal case, which has been assumed in this section, the power consumption in the melt pool, in a length  $dz$  of the channel, can be calculated from equation (8.7) (melt pumping), replacing the channel width,  $b$ , by the local melt pool width,  $b - X$ . Similarly, the power consumption in the clearance is given by equation (8.8), which was obtained assuming that the local pressure flow was small compared to drag flow. Rauwendaal (1990) computed  $E_{\mathrm{f}}$  for a power-law fluid. Tadmor and Klein (1970) adopted a different approach, and determined the power introduced in a film down-channel increment as the rate of energy introduced by viscous dissipation. This is given by:

$E_{\mathrm{f}} = [\text{Rate of energy introduced by viscous dissipation in the increment } \mathrm{dz}]$

$= \{\mathrm{[Heat~required~for~melting]}$  - [Heat conducted at the barrel surface]

$\times$  [Mass rate of melting]

The heat conducted at the barrel interface is obtained by differentiating equation (7.39):

$$
- X [ q ] _ {y = \delta_ {\mathrm {r}}} = \frac {X k _ {\mathrm {m}} \left(T _ {\mathrm {b}} - T _ {\mathrm {m}}\right)}{\delta_ {\mathrm {f}}} \tag {7.63}
$$

The mass rate of melting is  $Q_{x}\rho_{\mathrm{m}}\mathrm{d}z$ , where  $Q_{x} = \delta_{\mathrm{f}}V_{\mathrm{bx}} / 2$ . Therefore,

$$
\mathrm {d} E _ {\mathrm {f}} = \frac {\delta_ {\mathrm {f}} V _ {\mathrm {b x}} \rho_ {\mathrm {m}}}{2} \left[ C _ {\mathrm {m}} (\bar {T} - T _ {\mathrm {m}}) + C _ {\mathrm {p}} (T _ {\mathrm {m}} - T _ {\mathrm {p}}) + \Omega - \frac {X k _ {\mathrm {m}}}{\delta_ {\mathrm {f}}} \left(T _ {\mathrm {b}} - T _ {\mathrm {m}}\right) \right] \mathrm {d} z \tag {7.64}
$$
